Project 01 — Technical Depth
Critical technical decisions
and their reasons
01 — RobustScaler vs StandardScaler
Data Prep
Problem: The data has extreme outliers (income up to $3.8M, utilization 1.5). StandardScaler uses mean and standard deviation, which are sensitive to outliers.
Decision: RobustScaler uses the median and IQR, making it more resistant to outliers without sacrificing the interpretability of the linear transformation.
02 — Stratified Split (60/20/20)
Validation
Problem: The target class is imbalanced (93.2% good, 6.8% default). A random split risks producing an uneven distribution.
Decision: train_test_split(..., stratify=y) ensures each split has approximately 6.8% default, resulting in comparable metrics.
03 — SMOTE only on Train Set
Imbalance
Problem: Applying SMOTE before the split causes data leakage, as synthetic samples from the minority class will "leak" into the validation set.
Decision: SMOTE is fit and transformed only on the training set. Validation and test sets remain reflective of the imbalanced production distribution.
04 — Threshold Tuning via F1 Max
Business Logic
Problem: A 0.5 threshold is an arbitrary number that does not reflect asymmetric costs: a false negative (approving a defaulter) is far more expensive than a false positive (rejecting a good borrower).
Decision: Sweep the threshold on the validation set and select the one that maximizes the F1-minority score. Result: threshold 0.5354, saved as best_threshold.json.
05 — SHAP vs LIME
Explainability
Problem: Loan officers and regulators require accountable explanations, including for audit or regulatory compliance purposes.
Decision: SHAP uses Shapley values from game theory, which is theoretically stronger than LIME. It supports both local (per borrower) and global (feature importance across the entire dataset) explanations.

Feature Engineering — Business Rationale
weighted_late_score
late_30 × 1 + late_60 × 2 + late_90 × 4- 90-day late payments are a charge-off signal (banking industry standard). The 4× weight reflects a different level of severity.
income_per_dependent
An income of 5 million with 4 dependents means something very different from 5 million with no dependents. This feature captures family financial pressure that is not visible from raw income numbers alone.
dti_adjusted
Debt-to-income ratio is a banking regulatory standard (safe limit 30–40%), sso this feature allows the model to "speak the same language" as the risk manager.

Project 01 — Learnings & Takeaways
What I learned
and its limitations
Critical Learnings
1. "Perfect" results are a red flag
In early iterations, the model MAE was almost zero. I was skeptical, repeated the process, and found that the target variable could be mathematically derived from two features (tautology). This taught me that honest validation is more important than impressive metrics.
2. The threshold is a business decision, not a technical choice
I initially used a 0.5 threshold by default. After understanding the context of MSME credit more deeply, I realized the right threshold must reflect the asymmetric costs between false negatives and false positives, and must be adjustable without retraining.
3. Feature engineering from domain knowledge over statistical correlation
Adding weighted_late_score (which stems from the understanding that 90-day late payments are the charge-off limit in banking) provided more meaningful improvement than feature engineering based solely on correlation.
Acknowledged Limitations
• Data is from 2009 (financial crisis) — so the default rate might not represent current Indonesian MSME conditions.
• Recall is only 55% — meaning approximately 45% of defaults slip through the prediction. The model needs to be combined with manual review for borderline cases.
• Fairness audit has not been conducted — age as a feature has the potential to become a proxy for discrimination. A disparate impact audit is required before production deployment.
Project Summary
"Developed explainable credit risk model (86.4% AUC-ROC) with interactive policy dashboard; detected 55% of defaults pre-approval through XGBoost + SHAP pipeline with zero data leakage; operationalized decision threshold as version-controlled artifact enabling risk appetite simulation without model retraining"
Design Decision
Trade-off yang menentukan pilihan model:
"In this project, I chose XGBoost not because it had the highest AUC, but because its F1-minority score was 38% better than LightGBM. In credit scoring, a model that misses defaults is a business failure, even if its AUC is perfect
This insight came from understanding that the cost of a false negative, approving a borrower who later defaults, is far greater than the cost of a false positive, rejecting an actually eligible borrower. That is why I also saved the threshold as a separate artifact that the lending team can adjust without needing to call a data scientist."

Project 02 — Technical Challenge
Finding & solving
Mathematical Tautology
The Tautology Problem
First Iteration - It looked successful.
The first model with player-level aggregation produced a very low MAE. The predictions looked perfect; all players received a score around 0.71.
This was a red flag. After investigation, it was found that the target variable could be mathematically derived from two features:
y = nutmegs / total_dribbles = f(feature_1 / feature_2)
The model was not learning patterns; it was just doing arithmetic. This was a mathematical tautology.
Redesign: Player-Match Formulation
Before (Player-Level)
Each row = one player
Target = career nutmeg rate
Feature = career stats
Problem: y = f(x₁/x₂)
→
After (Player-Match)
Each row = one match
Target = nutmeg in match T
Feature = data before match T
Solution:: Predict the future from the past
shift(1) — Time-Series Integrity
Rolling features use shift(1) — meaning each row only uses information from previous matches, not the match being predicted.
rolling_nutmeg_rate_last5 = shift(1).rolling(5).mean()
First match for each player: rolling feature equals 0 (no prior history). This is methodologically honest.

Key Learnings
Data granularity determines model validity
Player-level aggregation looks simpler, but it results in tautology. The Player-Match formulation is more complex, but it is the only methodologically valid way for time-series prediction.
A small dataset is not a roadblock, but a design challenge
664 rows is a very small dataset for ML. The solution is not more data (since data is limited), but stricter regularization and more informative features.
Acknowledged limitations build greater trust
he Model Integrity page actually makes this project more convincing. Displaying weaknesses openly shows that I understand the model's limitations, not just how to build it.
Limitations & Development
• Currently only covers 3 international tournaments; domestic league data has not been used
• Players without history in these 3 competitions cannot yet be predicted.
• Dataset of 664 rows (currently being developed by expanding data coverage to further validate results)

Critical Technical Decisions
Separation of Operationa & Analytical
Analytical queries running on live data cause database lock contention and slow down transactions. Snapshots separate this load: transactions remain fast, and analytics do not compete with the POS
Manual Snapshot, Not Automated
The owner decides when to analyze (weekly, monthly, or ad-hoc). Automated snapshots would create too much unused data. Agency remains in the hands of the business owner.
MySQL Without ORM
Prepared statements provide full control over queries, better performance for prototypes, and built-in prevention of SQL injection. An ORM adds unnecessary abstraction at this phase.
Role-Based Dual Dashboard
Admins see real-time workflows. Owners see strategic trends. A single dashboard trying to serve both can create cognitive overload, making two dashboards with different contexts the more appropriate choice.

Critical Learnings
Good architecture solves business problems before technical problems
The separation of operational and analytical layers was born from the understanding that owners and staff have fundamentally different information needs, not from technical preference.
Immutability is a feature, not a limitation
An unchangeable snapshot might look like a limitation at first. But in the context of financial audits and decision accountability, immutability is a requirement, not a trade-off.
Manual-first is a choice, not a shortcoming
Inventory consumption is manually input by staff, not via IoT sensors. This is not a budget limitation, but a design decision: the system must be deployable today, in any laundry, without specialized hardware.
Project Summary
"Architected dual-layer laundry management system separating operational (real-time POS) from analytical (immutable snapshot-based analytics) concerns; computed 8 business KPIs with baseline comparison and significance detection; integrated local LLM (Gemma 3) for human-approved operational recommendations."
Decision Design
Choosing the right solution, not the most complex one:
"I briefly considered real-time analytics with data streaming. It is technically more impressive. But after understanding the business needs, a laundry owner does not need real-time data every second. An immutable snapshot is the right answer"
